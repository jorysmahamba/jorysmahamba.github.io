The Gauss-Bonnet Theorem: From Curves to Surfaces

Research Internship, LAMA UPEC (June - September 2025)
Differential Geometry Topology

The Gauss-Bonnet theorem stands as a cornerstone of differential geometry, revealing a profound link between local curvature and global topology. This report surveys the theorem's progression from the classical result for plane curves—where the total curvature of a simple closed curve equals 2π—to its full formulation for smooth surfaces. By establishing the topological invariance of the Euler characteristic through homological methods and proving Gauss's Theorema Egregium, we demonstrate that the integral of Gaussian curvature over a compact, orientable surface is determined solely by its topology: ∫X K dA = 2πχ(X). This work synthesizes these developments to provide a detailed, pedagogical proof of both the local and global versions of the theorem.

Mathematical Exploration of Phase Transitions in the Ising Model

Research Internship, LAMA UPEC (2024)
Probability Theory Statistical Physics

This work provides a rigorous mathematical introduction to the Ising model, a fundamental tool in statistical physics for understanding ferromagnetism. Moving beyond standard textbook treatments, this report focuses on the sharp contrast between one and two dimensions. We prove the absence of a phase transition in the 1D model and demonstrate its existence in the 2D case using Peierls' argument. The goal of this exposition is to bridge the gap between abstract probability theory and tangible physical phenomena, offering a self-contained resource for students seeking a deeper, mathematically precise understanding of statistical mechanics and phase transitions.

The Euler-Poincaré Characteristic of Surfaces: A Constructive Approach and Geometric Interpretation

June 2025
Topology Geometry Euler Characteristic
Author's Note: This post is a collection of notes I wrote while trying to understand the Euler characteristic on my own, prior to having sufficient knowledge to make everything precise in topology. It focuses on geometric intuition rather than rigor. Please read it as a conceptual vision rather than a formal mathematical treatise.
Abstract: I present a constructive decomposition method for computing the Euler-Poincaré characteristic of orientable surfaces of arbitrary genus. By decomposing higher genus surfaces into fundamental building blocks—which I term zero-pieces, one-pieces, and two-pieces—I derive the classical formula $\chi = 2 - 2g$ through explicit triangulation. This approach provides geometric intuition for the topological invariance of the Euler characteristic. I conclude with a geometric interpretation of $\chi$ as measuring the balance between degrees of freedom and topological constraints, inspired by the inflation dynamics of surfaces.

The Nobel Prize in Physics 2024: A Celebration of Machine Learning's Foundations in Physics

October 2024
Machine Learning Statistical Physics Nobel Prize
This article was written and reviewed for publication by the French Mathematical Society in the "Gazette" but was never published.

The 2024 Nobel Prize in Physics was awarded to Professors John J. Hopfield and Geoffrey E. Hinton for foundational discoveries enabling machine learning with artificial neural networks. This award highlights the profound role of physics—specifically statistical mechanics—in the development of modern AI.

Overview: In this article, I review the basics of Hopfield Networks—what they are, how they work, and why they are building blocks of modern machine learning architectures. I explore the deep analogy with the Ising model from statistical physics, which unveils the relationship between neural networks and physical systems.
